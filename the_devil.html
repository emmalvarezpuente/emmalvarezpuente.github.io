The Devil's Bridge 

To my Iris friend John O´Rourke

   There is a bridge that the devil made in one day without lime either sand. Here comes its history.

 

   Everything is due to the exhaustion of a humble maid in charge of taking the water to her gentleman's table, a Patrician whose house ran off with in the high of the city. The maid went up and down daily the sheer hills of the town with her pitcher until, one day, she invoked the devil in her help.

 

The devil come and he promised that he would make arrive the water to the house of her gentleman to change, of course, of the youth's soul. The maid only put one condition: that the matter was resolved before the exit of the sun, time in which she started to bring the water to the house.  

 

That night the devil summoned to his diabolical hordes to carry enormous stones, to carve, to polish and to lift the airy arches of the aqueduct. The roar was such that the Segovianos closed up terrified in their houses believing that a storm collapsed of the sky over their heads. 

 

It lacked a single stone in the central pilaster to finish the work; the devil brought it happy for the airs when oh! It started the song of the rooster announcing the dawn.  Surprised, the devil stopped their flight because the first sunbeam is advanced in the career. There was unfulfilled the condition imposed by the maid and he was left for it without her soul. 

 

The aqueduct of Segovia (Spain) is one of the most impressive engineering works that the Romans bequeathed us. So big, that the popular minds couldn’t attribute their construction to human hands. 

 

It was always believed that the aqueduct was built in the century I by order of the Trajano, Roman emperor born in Spain. In their time, a text embedded in the high of the stones remembered the date and the name of who ordered to lift the monument, but the characters disappeared leaving us only rake three arrays of holes caused by the pegs that held them centuries ago. To decipher the text through those vestiges became a challenge for the specialists.  

 

    Finally, in 1996, Géza Alföldy, of the University of Heidelberg, was able to figure out the enigma: Trajano only ordered the reconstruction of a monument existent. His work clarified the mystery but it opened another one: now it is not known who ordered their construction neither when it began.
